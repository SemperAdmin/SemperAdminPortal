## What Readiness Looks Like in Aviation

A ready aviation squadron has:

1. Aircraft material readiness sufficient to fly the missions assigned
2. Aircrew currency in type-model-series and required events
3. NATOPS evaluations not lapsed
4. Maintenance personnel qualified to the aircraft and the workload
5. Trained reservists or augmentees where applicable
6. ORM integration in every planning cycle

The CO does not personally execute most of these. The CO operates the levers driving them.

## The T&R Manual

NAVMC 3500.109A is the Aviation Training and Readiness Manual at the aviation-wide level. Platform-specific volumes (NAVMC 3500.110 series and adjacent) define the type-model-series-specific training and readiness events.

### What the T&R Manual contains

- Core skill training events (CSL events)
- Mission essential task list (METL) mapping
- Sustainment frequencies for each event
- Evaluation criteria
- Crew composition requirements
- Aircrew designation requirements
- Currency definitions
- Combat-readiness benchmarks

### How the T&R Manual drives the schedule

The CO does not write the flight schedule. The Operations Officer and schedule writers do. The T&R Manual is the constraint.

Every flight schedule is a translation of:

- T&R Manual requirements per aircrew
- Aircraft availability
- Mission requirements
- Training event prerequisites
- ORM constraints

A schedule violating T&R requirements (e.g., crew composition without qualified members) is invalid.

### CO-level discipline

The CO reviews the T&R Manual readiness picture at recurring Operations meetings. The questions:

- Who is approaching currency lapse?
- Who has not flown sufficient events for designation upgrade?
- Where are the gaps in instructor pilot depth?
- What does aircraft availability look like against the next 30, 60, 90 days?
- Which events are at risk due to weather or range availability?

The Aviation Commander's Mentorship Handbook captures the posture: "What gets measured gets performed." Aircrew readiness is what gets measured in aviation.

## NATOPS Evaluations

OPNAV M-3710.7 (NATOPS General Flight and Operating Instructions) sets the framework. Platform-specific NATOPS manuals define the specific evaluation procedures.

### NATOPS evaluation cadence

- Annual evaluation per aircrew per type-model-series
- Instrument check ride per platform-specific cadence
- Mission-specific evaluations (e.g., NVG, AAR, low-level) per local SOP
- Special qualification evaluations (Functional Check Flight, Maintenance Check Flight) per platform

### NATOPS Officer

The NATOPS Officer is the squadron-level steward of the evaluation program. Appointed by the CO. Coordinates with the FRS, MAWTS-1, and other squadrons for evaluator cross-training.

### When NATOPS evaluations lapse

- The affected aircrew is not authorized for the events in question
- ORM picture changes for the squadron
- DRRS-MC personnel readiness reflects the gap
- Flight schedule writers route around the lapse, compressing other aircrew workload

A squadron with multiple lapsed NATOPS evaluations is signaling a deeper issue: scheduling priority, evaluator capacity, or aircrew retention.

## FRS Qualification

The Fleet Replacement Squadron is the school producing newly designated aircrew in each type-model-series. A pilot or NFO arriving at the operational squadron has completed FRS.

### What FRS qualification means

- Basic competence in the platform
- NATOPS qualification at the entry level
- Initial designation as a Replacement Aircrew Member
- Eligibility for further squadron-level qualification (e.g., section lead, division lead, instructor pilot)

### What FRS does NOT mean

- Mission qualification for every squadron mission
- Currency in specific events (NVG, AAR, low-level, FCLP)
- Designator upgrade to instructor or evaluator
- Combat readiness

### CO-level discipline on aircrew progression

A new aircrew arriving from FRS is at the entry level. The CO ensures:

- Squadron-level training plan to bring the aircrew to combat-ready status
- Mentor pairing with senior aircrew
- ORM consideration in early mission assignment
- T&R event progression tracked

Compressing FRS aircrew progression to fill schedule gaps is a documented failure pattern.

## MAWTS-1 Integration

The Marine Aviation Weapons and Tactics Squadron One supports squadron-level training beyond producing Weapons and Tactics Instructors (WTIs).

### What MAWTS-1 offers a squadron

- Academic instructor support for unit training
- Additional instructor depth for specific events
- ACE integration expertise for MAGTF planning
- Mishap review and lessons learned
- Tactics and TTPs updates
- Doctrine integration support

The Aviation Commander's Mentorship Handbook directly addresses this:

"Use MAWTS. It is not only for the WTI class. It is your weapons school and your source for academics, additional instructors, and ACE integration expertise."

### CO-level discipline

A CO who calls MAWTS-1 only for WTI nominations misses 80 percent of the available support. Schedule academic sessions, request specific instructor support, leverage MAWTS for ACE integration planning.

The MAWTS-1 connection is fragile if not maintained. A new CO inheriting a squadron with limited MAWTS engagement rebuilds the connection deliberately.

## ACE Integration

The Aviation Combat Element of the MAGTF is the integrated air package. Squadron commanders contribute to ACE through:

- ACE-wide training events
- Mission planning respecting ACE priorities
- Cross-squadron coordination
- MAWTS-1 ACE-level integration support
- Wing and Group-level operations participation

The Aviation Commander's Mentorship Handbook emphasizes ACE-level thinking even at squadron command level.

## DRRS-MC Reporting for Aviation

The Defense Readiness Reporting System Marine Corps (DRRS-MC) report from an aviation unit covers the same C/P/S/R/T levels as ground units, with aviation-specific commander remarks.

### Aviation-specific remarks

- Aircraft material readiness rate
- Aircrew currency picture
- NATOPS evaluation status
- Maintenance personnel qualification posture
- Training event completion against T&R Manual
- Operational tempo and crew rest considerations

### Subjective override discipline

When the CO overrides a category, the rationale is documented in remarks. Common aviation overrides:

- P-Level (personnel) impacted by aircrew currency more than headcount
- S-Level (supply) impacted by repair-part availability for specific platform
- R-Level (training) impacted by range or weather rather than personnel
- T-Level (equipment condition) impacted by depot rotation timing

## Quarterly Aviation-Specific Reviews

Aviation commands add specific reviews to the standard quarterly battle rhythm.

| Quarterly review | What it covers |
|-------------------|----------------|
| Aircrew currency board | Every aircrew member reviewed for currency picture |
| Maintenance qualification review | Personnel qualifications mapped to workload |
| Aviation Safety Awareness Council | Monthly minimum but quarterly comprehensive review |
| Aircraft material status review | Trend analysis with Maintenance Officer |
| Aviation Wing or Group readiness review | Higher-headquarters coordination |

## Common Failures in Readiness and Training

1. **T&R Manual treated as administrative tracker, not operational driver.** Schedule built around aircraft availability without T&R event progression.
2. **NATOPS lapses tolerated.** Aircrew flying with expired evaluations. Procedural violation.
3. **FRS aircrew assigned mission-qualified work prematurely.** Compresses learning curve, increases risk.
4. **MAWTS-1 underused.** Handbook flags this directly.
5. **DRRS-MC remarks blank on subjective overrides.** MCO 3000.13B violation.
6. **Aircrew currency board not run quarterly.** Visibility into individual progression lost.
7. **Maintenance qualification stale.** Personnel performing work outside their qualification.
8. **Training events deferred without ORM analysis.** Cascading effect on combat readiness.

## Decision Framework for Schedule Approval

Before signing off on the monthly or weekly flight schedule, the CO confirms:

1. Aircrew assignments respect NATOPS currency for each event
2. Crew composition meets T&R Manual requirements
3. Mission qualification confirmed before mission assignment
4. ORM analysis present for non-routine events
5. Aircraft material status supports the schedule
6. No FRS aircrew assigned beyond their progression
7. Weather and range availability confirmed
8. Crew rest discipline maintained

A schedule failing on any of these is sent back for adjustment before approval.
