## Why This Is Your First Responsibility

Per MCO 5000.14D<Citation source="MCO 5000.14D" /> Enclosure 3 paragraph a, the first listed commander responsibility for personnel administration is establishing command procedures for receiving IPAC support and ensuring connectivity with the IPAC. The MCAP states it directly: "For administration to function efficiently, free and open exchange of information must exist between the unit and the IPAC."

This is not delegated to S-1. You as commander own the relationship. S-1 executes within the procedures you establish.

## Why the Relationship Matters

The 1999 consolidation moved most personnel administrative work from operating force units to IPACs in the supporting establishment. Per MCO 5000.14D<Citation source="MCO 5000.14D" />, this reduced commanders' direct access to personnel information and processes. The IPAC supports you by relieving the burden of higher-level admin functions, but only if the relationship works.

A broken IPAC relationship creates these problems.

- Pay errors take weeks to fix instead of days
- NAVMC 10922<Citation source="NAVMC 10922" /> packages get rejected without clear reason
- Separation packages stall at PAC routing
- Audit findings pile up at the unit level
- IG complaints against the unit increase
- Marines lose trust in the chain

A working IPAC relationship prevents all of this.

## Identifying Your Supporting IPAC

If you do not know which IPAC supports your unit, find out today.

- Geographic alignment is typical (your IPAC is on or near your base).
- Some units have non-standard IPAC alignment (deployed units, training commands, recruiting).
- Marine Corps Bases each have an IPAC. Some have multiple branches.
- Reserve units are supported by I&I staff, not IPACs in the same way.

Your S-1 should know. If they do not, that is your first finding.

## Required Points of Contact

Get these names, phone numbers, and emails on file.

| IPAC Position | What They Handle |
|---------------|-----------------|
| OIC IPAC | Overall coordination and escalation |
| Personnel Officer (0170) | Complex pay and separations cases |
| Joins Section Head | Inbound members and ESR creation |
| Pay Section Head | BAH, BAS, special pays, audits |
| Separations Section Head | Discharge processing, DD-214 |
| Travel Section Head | DTS, vouchers, mileage claims |
| UD/MIPS Section Head | Diary processing and corrections |

Maintain this contact list at S-1. Update quarterly or when IPAC personnel rotate.

## Establishing Command Procedures

Per MCO 5000.14D<Citation source="MCO 5000.14D" />, you establish the procedures. Document them in writing as a unit SOP. Cover these areas.

### Routing of Standard Actions

Define which actions route directly from S-1 to PAC vs which require commander review first.

- Routine pay corrections: S-1 routes directly to PAC
- BAH changes from NAVMC 10922<Citation source="NAVMC 10922" />: S-1 routes directly
- NJP results: Commander signs first, then S-1 to PAC
- Administrative separations: Commander recommendation first, then to PAC
- Awards: Approval routing per command policy, then to PAC

### Liaison Personnel

Designate specific Marines who interface with IPAC.

- S-1 Officer or Adjutant (primary)
- Admin Chief (daily working contact)
- Casualty Assistance Officer (when applicable)
- SJA (legal coordination)

### Communication Cadence

Establish regular touchpoints.

- Weekly: Admin Chief calls IPAC counterpart on open issues
- Monthly: S-1 Officer meets with IPAC OIC on metrics and trends
- Quarterly: Commander touches base with IPAC OIC on relationship health
- Annually: Joint review of audit findings and process improvements

### Escalation Paths

When something stalls, define the path.

1. S-1 admin clerk to PAC counterpart (working level)
2. Admin Chief to PAC Section Head (NCOIC level)
3. S-1 Officer to PAC Personnel Officer (0170)
4. Commander to IPAC OIC
5. Commander up the chain to MCAAT (ask_mcaat@usmc.mil) for policy disputes

## Tracking IPAC Performance

Hold IPAC accountable through metrics. Track these monthly.

| Metric | Target | What It Tells You |
|--------|--------|-------------------|
| NAVMC 10922<Citation source="NAVMC 10922" /> processing time | < 10 working days | Life event responsiveness |
| Pay error resolution time | < 5 working days | Working relationship health |
| Separation package processing | < 30 days from submission | Discharge backlog |
| DTS voucher processing | < 5 working days | Travel reimbursement speed |
| ESR audit completion | 100% on cycle | Records management |

Share these monthly with your IPAC OIC. Trends going wrong is your early warning.

## What You Owe IPAC

The relationship is two-way. Ensure your unit delivers these to IPAC.

- Complete packages with all supporting documents (no incomplete submissions)
- Accurate UMSR submissions via MOL (commander responsibility per MCO 5000.14D<Citation source="MCO 5000.14D" />)
- Timely Pro/Con marks and counseling Page 11s
- Clear unit diary submissions for promotions, transfers, awards
- LIMDU documents forwarded immediately
- PEB tracking documentation
- Meal card management
- Fitness reports processed on time
- Command payroll review monthly with discrepancies reported

A unit that delivers clean inputs gets faster IPAC service. A unit that submits incomplete packages gets pushed to the bottom of the queue.

## Common Relationship Failures

These break the IPAC relationship.

1. **Going around IPAC to HQMC or DFAS.** This burns relationships. Use IPAC first.
2. **Repeated incomplete submissions.** PAC clerks remember units that send junk packages.
3. **Public criticism of IPAC at command meetings.** Address performance privately with IPAC OIC.
4. **Skipping the chain on escalations.** Going direct to MCAAT without trying IPAC first.
5. **No commander engagement.** Letting S-1 handle everything without commander interface.

## When the Relationship Breaks

If IPAC service fails repeatedly.

1. Document specific incidents with dates, times, and actions.
2. Schedule a face-to-face with IPAC OIC. Bring documentation.
3. Develop a corrective action plan jointly.
4. If unresolved, escalate up your chain to your superior commander.
5. As a last resort, submit through your chain to MCAAT for policy review.

Do not let it fester. Marines pay the price.

## Decision Framework

For any IPAC issue, ask these questions.

- Has my S-1 attempted resolution at the working level first?
- Is this a one-time issue or a pattern?
- Does the issue require commander engagement, or can S-1 resolve?
- Have I documented the issue for the IPAC OIC discussion?
- What outcome do I want from the engagement?

Engage personally when the pattern persists, when Marines are being harmed, or when policy clarity is needed.

## Related Roles

The IPAC relationship affects everyone in the unit.

- **Marines** experience the IPAC relationship through their own pay and records actions.
- **Leaders** route their Marines' concerns through S-1 to IPAC.
- **Admin** at S-1 lives the relationship daily and is your eyes on IPAC performance.
