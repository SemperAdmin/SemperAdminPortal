## FTAP and STAP

| Program | Audience | Typical Window |
|---|---|---|
| First Term Alignment Plan (FTAP) | First-term Marines (typically L/Cpl to Sgt) | 18 to 6 months from EAS |
| Subsequent Term Alignment Plan (STAP) | Career Marines (typically SNCO) | 18 to 6 months from EAS |

Active MARADMINs adjust the program detail. Pull the current FTAP or STAP MARADMIN before any reenlistment package.

<Callout variant="warning" title="MARADMIN currency">
FTAP and STAP MARADMINs update annually or when policy shifts. Confirm the active MARADMIN with S-1 or the career planner before relying on specific procedural detail.
</Callout>

## The 18-Month Conversation

At 18 months from EAS, open the conversation.

- Where do you see yourself in 5 years?
- What has the Corps given you that you want to continue?
- What do you want next that requires reenlistment to achieve?
- What does your family think?
- What concerns are sitting on the decision?

The 18-month conversation surfaces intent. The Marine starts thinking. The leader starts coordinating.

## The 12-Month Conversation

At 12 months from EAS, coordinate with the career planner.

- Career planner brief the Marine on eligibility, MOS health, bonuses, schools.
- You support the Marine through the formal conversation.
- The Marine starts seeing the actual options.
- Decision starts to form.

A Marine first hearing about reenlistment options at 6 months has compressed decision time.

## The 6-Month Conversation

At 6 months from EAS, the package builds.

- Reenlistment package routes through the chain.
- Approval lands typically 60 to 90 days before EAS.
- Marine commits or declines.

Your role at this stage supports the decision the Marine has made.

## What Drives Reenlistment Decisions

Marines reenlist for.

- Continued service to Corps and country.
- Career path inside the Corps.
- Bonus or specific incentive (SRB, school, duty station).
- Family stability through continued benefits.
- No clear civilian alternative.

Marines decline for.

- Civilian opportunity outside the Corps.
- Family decision (spouse career, education, geography).
- Burnout from operational tempo.
- Policy or culture concerns.
- Fit issues that did not resolve.

The leader hears both sides. The leader does not push the Corps' interest over the Marine's interest.

## When the Marine Wants to Reenlist But Cannot

Sometimes eligibility blocks reenlistment.

- MOS over-strength.
- Performance issues blocking the package.
- Medical condition not fitting for continued service.
- Disciplinary history affecting eligibility.

Coordinate with career planner on alternatives.

- Lateral move to an open MOS.
- Performance improvement plan with a re-look.
- Medical evaluation and waiver process.
- Alternative service paths (reserve component).

A Marine who wants to reenlist deserves the alternatives surfaced.

## When the Marine Wants to Leave

Support the decision.

- Coordinate transition planning early.
- Connect the Marine with TRP at 365 days from EAS.
- Coordinate education and credential planning.
- Support the GI Bill or transition use.

A Marine planning a thoughtful transition serves the Corps after EAS as a positive ambassador. A Marine pushed out poorly carries that experience for life.

## Documenting Intent

In the leader notebook.

- Stated intent at 18 months.
- Coordinated at 12 months.
- Decision at 6 months.
- Final outcome.

Update at every milestone.

## Common Reenlistment Conversation Failures

- 18-month conversation never happened. Marine surprised at 12 months.
- Career planner brief skipped or delegated entirely without leader support.
- Marine pressured toward reenlistment without honest exploration.
- Marine deciding to leave treated as a section failure.
- No coordination with TRP for departing Marines.
