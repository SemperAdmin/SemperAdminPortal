## The Four-Level Escalation Ladder

| Level | Who | When |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Named S-1 contact | Initial routing and follow-up |
| 2 | S-1 chief or OIC | Contact unresponsive or initial denial |
| 3 | Battalion or higher S-1 | Section S-1 cannot resolve |
| 4 | Through the chain to commander | Structural or policy issue |

Each level needs the same package and a clear ask. Skipping levels burns the relationship and forfeits the lower-level fix path.

## Before You Escalate

Run a self-check.

- Is the package actually complete?
- Are signatures and certifications correct?
- Is the time window right?
- Has the standard processing window actually elapsed?
- Have I given the named contact a fair chance to respond?

A premature escalation reads as a leader who does not understand the process. The S-1 remembers.

## Document the Denial Basis

When S-1 says no, capture the basis in writing.

- Date.
- Named contact.
- Specific reason given.
- Policy reference if cited.

A denial with a written basis allows the next level to evaluate. A denial captured only verbally allows the next level to default to "S-1 has reasons."

## Level 2 - The S-1 Chief

If the named contact is unresponsive or the denial appears wrong, walk to the S-1 chief or OIC.

- Bring the package and the documented denial basis.
- State the ask clearly. "I need this package to clear by X date."
- Listen to the chief's response.
- If the chief sustains the denial, ask for the policy basis in writing.

Most level-2 escalations resolve here. The chief either unblocks or shows you the policy reason.

## Level 3 - Battalion or Higher S-1

A persistent denial at level 2 with no clear policy basis warrants the next level. Coordinate with your chain first.

- Brief your immediate senior on the issue.
- Get authorization to engage the next level S-1.
- Walk the package and the denial chain to the higher S-1.
- Let the higher S-1 work the relationship with the lower S-1.

A bypass without authorization is a process foul. Get the chain involved.

## Level 4 - Commander Engagement

A structural or policy issue with no resolution at level 3 warrants commander engagement.

- Brief the commander with documentation.
- The commander engages peer commanders or the chain.
- The fix may run through policy update rather than this specific package.

Level 4 is rare. Most packages clear at level 1 or level 2. Reserve level 4 for genuine structural problems.

## When the Denial Is Correct

Sometimes S-1 says no for a real policy reason.

- The package does not meet the criteria.
- The time window has expired beyond recovery.
- The Marine's record does not support the action.
- A higher policy blocks the action.

A correct denial is a denial. Brief the Marine, document the closure, and pivot to alternatives. Walking a package up the chain that should not be walked burns relationships and teaches Marines the wrong lesson about the system.

## After the Escalation

Document the resolution in the leader notebook.

- Initial submission.
- Each escalation step.
- Final resolution.
- Lessons for future packages.

The next time a similar package routes, you have the recovery path documented.
