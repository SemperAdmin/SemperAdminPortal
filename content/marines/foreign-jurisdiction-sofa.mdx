## Start Here If You Are New to Foreign Jurisdiction and SOFA

When you are stationed OCONUS or traveling OCONUS on official orders, you are subject to the laws of the host nation AND the Uniform Code of Military Justice (UCMJ). The Status of Forces Agreement (SOFA) between the United States and the host nation governs which jurisdiction applies when a Marine commits an offense overseas. Generally, the U.S. has primary jurisdiction over offenses against the U.S. (e.g., UCMJ violations) and offenses involving only U.S. personnel. The host nation often has primary jurisdiction over offenses against host nation persons or property. Marines arrested overseas should immediately contact JAG and the U.S. Embassy or Consulate. Pre-departure legal briefings should be attended before any OCONUS PCS so you understand the specific SOFA that applies. Common OCONUS Marine locations are governed by specific SOFAs. U.S.-Japan SOFA, U.S.-Korea SOFA, NATO SOFA (Germany, Italy, etc.). Per MCO 5800.16 Vol 16<Citation source="MCO 5800.16 Vol 16" /> and applicable SOFAs.

## Why This Matters

Marines stationed OCONUS are subject to TWO legal systems.

- The Uniform Code of Military Justice (UCMJ) - the U.S. military criminal code.
- The criminal law of the host nation - the laws of the country where you are stationed.

Both systems can apply simultaneously. The SOFA helps allocate which system has primary jurisdiction in different situations.

## What Is a Status of Forces Agreement (SOFA)

A SOFA is a treaty or executive agreement between the United States and a host nation that governs the legal status of U.S. military personnel and their dependents present in that nation.

### What SOFAs Address

- Criminal jurisdiction over U.S. personnel.
- Civil jurisdiction.
- Tax exemptions for U.S. personnel.
- Customs and import privileges.
- Driving and vehicle registration.
- Other administrative matters.

### Why SOFAs Exist

SOFAs prevent confusion and conflict between U.S. military operations and host nation laws. They provide stability and predictability.

## Specific SOFAs for Common Marine Locations

### U.S.-Japan SOFA

Governs U.S. military presence in Japan. Marine Corps presence is significant on Okinawa and at Iwakuni.

#### Key Features

- Specific procedures for criminal investigations involving U.S. personnel.
- Detailed jurisdictional allocation.
- Specific provisions for Okinawa given the long-standing U.S. presence.

### U.S.-Korea SOFA

Governs U.S. military presence in the Republic of Korea. Marine Corps presence involves periodic training.

#### Key Features

- Specific procedures for criminal investigations.
- Allocation of jurisdiction based on offense type and parties involved.

### NATO SOFA

Covers U.S. forces in NATO countries including Germany, Italy, Spain, and others.

#### Key Features

- Standardized framework for all NATO members.
- Bilateral supplementary agreements for specific countries (e.g., the Germany Supplementary Agreement).

### Other Country SOFAs

Marine Corps deployments to other countries are governed by specific bilateral SOFAs or operational agreements. The Foreign Criminal Jurisdiction Officer (FCJO) at the unit can provide specifics.

## Primary vs Concurrent Jurisdiction

### Concurrent Jurisdiction

Both the U.S. (under UCMJ) and the host nation may have jurisdiction over an offense. SOFAs typically allocate primary jurisdiction.

### Primary Jurisdiction (U.S.)

The U.S. has primary jurisdiction in cases including.

- Offenses solely against the U.S. or U.S. personnel.
- Offenses arising from the performance of official duty.
- Many UCMJ-specific offenses without host nation parallel.

### Primary Jurisdiction (Host Nation)

The host nation has primary jurisdiction in cases including.

- Offenses against host nation persons or property.
- Offenses involving host nation citizens.
- Offenses against host nation security or order.

### Waiver of Primary Jurisdiction

In many cases, a country with primary jurisdiction may waive jurisdiction in favor of the other country. SOFAs include procedures for jurisdiction waiver requests.

## What to Do if Arrested or Detained Overseas

### Step 1. Comply With the Arresting Authority

Cooperate physically. Do NOT resist arrest. Do NOT flee.

### Step 2. Identify Yourself as U.S. Military

Show your military ID. State that you are a U.S. service member.

### Step 3. Request U.S. Embassy or Consulate Notification

Under the Vienna Convention on Consular Relations, you have the right to consular notification. Request that the U.S. Embassy or Consulate be notified of your detention.

### Step 4. Request JAG Notification

Ask that your unit JAG and chain of command be notified.

### Step 5. Do NOT Make Statements Without Counsel

Do NOT answer substantive questions about the alleged offense without legal counsel. Article 31 rights (and the U.S. constitutional right to counsel) protect you.

### Step 6. Be Patient

The process of consular notification, JAG involvement, and U.S. Embassy intervention takes time. Stay calm and do not antagonize host nation authorities.

## The Foreign Criminal Jurisdiction Officer (FCJO)

The Marine Corps assigns FCJOs at OCONUS commands to coordinate foreign jurisdiction matters.

### What the FCJO Does

- Coordinates with host nation authorities.
- Coordinates with the U.S. Embassy or Consulate.
- Coordinates with JAG.
- Tracks Marines facing host nation legal action.
- Provides updates to the chain of command.
- Coordinates jurisdiction waiver requests.

### Reaching the FCJO

Through your unit S-1, JAG, or directly. The FCJO contact information is posted at OCONUS commands.

## The U.S. Embassy and Consulate

### Consular Services

The U.S. Embassy or Consulate provides services to U.S. citizens detained abroad. Services include.

- Visiting the detainee.
- Providing a list of local attorneys.
- Notifying family.
- Facilitating communication with U.S. authorities.

### Consular Limitations

The U.S. Embassy CANNOT.

- Get you out of jail.
- Provide legal representation.
- Pay fines or post bail (in most cases).
- Override host nation legal procedures.

### Coordination With JAG

For Marines, consular services coordinate with the unit JAG and the FCJO.

## Differences in Legal Systems

Host nation legal systems can differ significantly from the U.S. system.

### Procedural Differences

- Some countries do not have a presumption of innocence in the same way as the U.S.
- Some countries have inquisitorial rather than adversarial systems.
- Some countries do not have jury trials.
- Pretrial detention practices vary.

### Substantive Differences

- Conduct legal in the U.S. may be illegal in the host nation (e.g., possession of certain items, public conduct).
- Severity of penalties may differ significantly.
- Drug offenses are often punished much more severely in many host nations.

### Why Pre-Departure Briefings Matter

The pre-departure legal briefing covers the specific differences for the destination country. Attend it.

## Pre-Departure Legal Briefings

### What the Briefing Covers

- Specific SOFA framework for the destination.
- Local laws Marines should know about.
- Cultural differences affecting legal exposure.
- Common offense scenarios at the destination.
- How to avoid legal trouble.
- What to do if arrested.

### When the Briefing Occurs

- Before deployment to OCONUS.
- Before PCS to OCONUS.
- Periodically updated for permanent OCONUS units.

### Coordinated By

JAG, FCJO, and command S-1.

## Common Foreign Jurisdiction Scenarios

### Scenario 1. DUI in Japan

Marine arrested for DUI in Okinawa or Iwakuni.

- Japan has very strict DUI laws.
- Japanese police investigate.
- The U.S.-Japan SOFA governs jurisdictional allocation.
- Coordinate with FCJO and JAG immediately.
- The U.S. may request waiver of jurisdiction in some cases.

### Scenario 2. Assault Off Base in Korea

Marine involved in fight off base in Seoul or Busan.

- Korean police investigate.
- The U.S.-Korea SOFA governs.
- The Korean prosecutor decides on charges.
- The U.S. may request waiver.
- JAG and FCJO coordinate.

### Scenario 3. Drug Possession in Germany

Marine found with controlled substance in Germany.

- German law applies.
- German penalties for drug possession can be significant.
- The NATO SOFA governs.
- JAG and FCJO coordinate.
- The U.S. may request waiver but Germany retains primary jurisdiction over many drug offenses.

### Scenario 4. Off-Duty Vehicle Accident in Italy

Marine involved in vehicle accident off base in Italy.

- Italian police investigate.
- Italian civil and criminal law may apply.
- The NATO SOFA governs.
- JAG and FCJO coordinate.

### Scenario 5. Dependent Issue Overseas

Marine's dependent has legal trouble overseas.

- SOFA may apply to dependents.
- JAG personal services attorney can advise.
- U.S. Embassy can provide consular services to the dependent.

## What U.S. Military Authorities Can and Cannot Do

### Can Do

- Coordinate with host nation authorities.
- Request waiver of host nation jurisdiction.
- Provide UCMJ jurisdiction in cases where U.S. has authority.
- Provide JAG and Defense Counsel for U.S. proceedings.
- Coordinate with U.S. Embassy or Consulate.

### Cannot Do

- Override host nation jurisdiction.
- Exempt Marines from host nation laws.
- Get Marines out of host nation custody at will.
- Stop host nation prosecutions.

## Differences When Off-Base vs On-Base

### On-Base Conduct

When a Marine is on a U.S. military installation overseas, the SOFA generally provides U.S. authority. Host nation may have limited access or jurisdiction.

### Off-Base Conduct

When a Marine is off base in the host nation, the host nation generally has primary authority subject to SOFA limitations.

### Why This Matters

Off-base conduct creates significantly more exposure to host nation jurisdiction. Marines should be especially careful off base.

## Cultural and Legal Awareness

### Common Issues for Marines Overseas

- DUI (penalties often severe).
- Drug possession (penalties often severe).
- Public intoxication (cultural and legal expectations vary).
- Conduct that would be minor in the U.S. but is significant in host nation (e.g., insulting officials, public displays).
- Failure to register vehicles or dependents per host nation requirements.

### Cultural Sensitivity

Beyond legal exposure, Marines represent the United States. Conduct affecting host nation perception of U.S. forces has strategic implications. Marines are expected to be ambassadors of the U.S.

## Common Questions Marines Ask

What is a SOFA?

Status of Forces Agreement. A treaty or executive agreement between the U.S. and a host nation governing the legal status of U.S. military personnel present in that nation.

When am I subject to host nation laws?

Generally any time you are present in the host nation. The SOFA may provide certain protections but does not exempt you from host nation laws.

When does the U.S. have primary jurisdiction?

Generally for offenses against the U.S. or U.S. personnel and offenses arising from the performance of official duty.

When does the host nation have primary jurisdiction?

Generally for offenses against host nation persons or property and offenses involving host nation citizens.

What do I do if arrested overseas?

Comply with the arresting authority. Identify yourself as U.S. military. Request U.S. Embassy or Consulate notification. Request JAG notification. Do NOT make statements without counsel.

Can the U.S. military get me out of host nation custody?

The U.S. may request waiver of host nation jurisdiction in some cases. The U.S. cannot override host nation jurisdiction. The host nation decides.

What is the FCJO?

Foreign Criminal Jurisdiction Officer. Marine Corps officer at OCONUS commands who coordinates foreign jurisdiction matters.

What does the U.S. Embassy do for me?

Consular services. Visit, provide attorney list, notify family, facilitate communication. Cannot get you out of jail or provide legal representation.

Do I have rights similar to my U.S. constitutional rights overseas?

Article 31 rights apply to U.S. military investigators. Host nation rights depend on host nation law (which may differ significantly from U.S. constitutional protections).

What is the pre-departure legal briefing?

A briefing before OCONUS PCS or deployment covering the specific SOFA framework, local laws, cultural differences, and what to do if arrested. Attend it.

Are penalties for offenses overseas the same as in the U.S.?

Often no. Many offenses (especially DUI and drug offenses) carry significantly more severe penalties in host nations.

Does SOFA apply to dependents?

Often yes. The specific application depends on the SOFA and the offense type. JAG can advise.

What if my conduct is legal in the U.S. but illegal in the host nation?

Host nation law applies. Pre-departure briefing should cover specific differences for the destination.

## Where to Go for Help

### Routing by Issue

- Immediate emergency overseas. U.S. Embassy or Consulate at the host nation.
- Foreign Criminal Jurisdiction Officer (FCJO). Through OCONUS unit S-1 or JAG.
- Personal Services Attorney for general questions about OCONUS legal matters. Installation LSSS or OSJA.
- Defense Counsel for U.S. military proceedings related to overseas events. JAG Defense Counsel.
- Consular services for detained dependents. U.S. Embassy or Consulate.
- U.S. State Department country information. https://travel.state.gov/.
- Pre-departure legal briefing. Coordinate with command and JAG before OCONUS movement.
