## Start Here If You Are New to the Performance Evaluation System

The Marine Corps evaluates every Marine through one of two systems based on grade. The Fitness Report (FITREP) per MCO 1610.7B<Citation source="MCO 1610.7B" /> covers Sergeants and above. The Junior Enlisted Performance Evaluation System (JEPES) per MCO 1616.1<Citation source="MCO 1616.1" /> covers E-1 through E-4. Both systems document performance, character, and potential. Both feed into promotion boards, retention decisions, special duty selection, and assignment monitor decisions. The Performance Evaluation Section (PES) at HQMC Manpower and Reserve Affairs runs the program. Key stakeholder roles include the Marine Reported On (MRO), the Reporting Senior (RS), and the Reviewing Officer (RO). Recent updates include MARADMIN 066/26<Citation source="MARADMIN 066/26" /> effective 1 January 2026 establishing the waist-to-height ratio (WHtR) body composition framework that drives Section A Item 8 reporting on FITREPs. Per MCO 1610.7B<Citation source="MCO 1610.7B" />, MCO 1616.1<Citation source="MCO 1616.1" />, MARADMIN 066/26<Citation source="MARADMIN 066/26" />.

## Two Systems Based on Grade

### FITREP for Sergeants and Above

Per MCO 1610.7B<Citation source="MCO 1610.7B" />, FITREPs document the performance of all Marines E-5 (Sergeant) and above and all Officers (W-1 through O-10).

- Standard reporting periods cover specific date ranges.
- Multiple report types capture different evaluation events.
- The MRO is rated by the Reporting Senior and reviewed by the Reviewing Officer.
- The Master Brief Sheet (MBS) aggregates all FITREPs across the Marine's career.

### JEPES for E-1 Through E-4

Per MCO 1616.1<Citation source="MCO 1616.1" />, JEPES documents the performance of all junior enlisted Marines E-1 (Private) through E-4 (Corporal).

- Replaces the legacy proficiency and conduct (Pro/Con) marks framework.
- Designed for the leadership chain that observes and supervises junior enlisted day-to-day.
- Captures performance attributes appropriate to the junior enlisted role.
- Feeds into Sergeant promotion eligibility.

## Why Performance Evaluations Matter

### Promotion

Performance evaluations are the single most important driver of promotion above the rank of Corporal. Promotion boards review the MBS (FITREPs) for SNCOs and Officers. JEPES evaluations support the cutting score and promotion pathway for E-1 through E-4.

### Retention and Reenlistment

Retention decisions, reenlistment bonuses, and lateral move approvals review performance evaluations. A pattern of strong evaluations supports retention. A pattern of weak or adverse evaluations affects retention eligibility.

### Special Duty Selection

Special Duty Assignment selection (Recruiting Duty, Drill Instructor Duty, Marine Security Guard, MARSOC, Career Recruiter, etc.) reviews FITREPs. The B-billet selection process explicitly cites FITREP performance as a primary factor.

### Assignment Monitors

MMEA and MMOA monitors use performance evaluations during PCS slating. Strong performers get more competitive assignments. The MBS profile signals the Marine's capability.

### Adverse Administrative Actions

Patterns of poor performance documented through performance evaluations support administrative separation, reduction in grade, and other adverse actions.

## The Performance Evaluation Section (PES) at MMPB-23

### PES Mission

The Performance Evaluation Section at HQMC Manpower and Reserve Affairs (MMPB-23) runs the Marine Corps performance evaluation program. PES.

- Maintains MCO 1610.7B<Citation source="MCO 1610.7B" /> and MCO 1616.1<Citation source="MCO 1616.1" />.
- Operates the Performance Evaluation Review Board (PERB) for FITREP appeals.
- Provides program-level escalation for Reporting Seniors and Reviewing Officers.
- Coordinates with MMEA, MMOA, MMSR-6 (Reserve), and TECOM.

### MMPB-23 Three Sections

MMPB-23 operates through three sections.

- Policy, Compliance and Corrections. Administrative corrections, IGMC inspections, Commander's Timeliness Reports.
- Processing and Audit. Receives roughly 2,000 reports daily and routes errors to Audit.
- JEPES. Administers the junior enlisted system per MCO 1616.1<Citation source="MCO 1616.1" />.

The A-PES and FITREP Administration leaf covers MMPB-23 operations in detail.

## Core Roles

### Marine Reported On (MRO)

The Marine being evaluated. The MRO has rights and responsibilities.

- Right to receive an initial counseling within 30 days of reporting (per the Joining a Unit page).
- Right to mid-period counseling.
- Right to acknowledge the FITREP before submission.
- Right to submit an MRO addendum on adverse reports.
- Right to appeal to the PERB on substantive errors.
- Responsibility to maintain personal records reflecting current performance.
- Responsibility to verify MBS accuracy regularly.

### Reporting Senior (RS)

The Marine writing the report. Typically.

- For company-grade officers and SNCOs, the immediate or designated commander.
- For E-5 and E-6, the platoon commander or section leader.
- For O-3 and below, the Battalion Commander or equivalent.

The RS responsibilities. Initial and mid-period counseling, accurate observation of MRO performance, completing the FITREP within MCO 1610.7B<Citation source="MCO 1610.7B" /> requirements, marking honestly with comparative assessment, and submitting on time.

### Reviewing Officer (RO)

The senior officer who reviews the RS's report. Typically the RS's immediate commander (e.g., Battalion Commander reviews Company Commanders).

The RO responsibilities. Concurrence or non-concurrence with the RS, third officer sighter triggers, profile management, fairness and consistency oversight.

The Reporting Senior and Reviewing Officer Roles leaf covers RS and RO duties in detail.

## How a Performance Evaluation Flows

### FITREP Flow

1. Reporting period begins. RS conducts initial counseling within the JTR-equivalent window per MCO 1610.7B<Citation source="MCO 1610.7B" />.
2. Mid-period counseling at the midpoint.
3. Reporting period ends. RS drafts the FITREP.
4. RS submits to RO for review.
5. RO concurs (signs) or non-concurs (initiates additional process).
6. MRO acknowledges receipt.
7. FITREP submitted to PES via the Marine Corps Total Force System (MCTFS).
8. Report appears on the Marine's MBS.

### JEPES Flow

1. Reporting period begins. Reporting Official observes the junior Marine.
2. Reporting period ends. Reporting Official prepares the JEPES report.
3. Report submitted through the chain.
4. Marine acknowledges.
5. Report enters the personnel record.

The FITREP Reporting Cycle and JEPES leaves cover the flows in detail.

## Body Composition Reporting Integration

Per MARADMIN 066/26<Citation source="MARADMIN 066/26" /> effective 1 January 2026, body composition information is reported in FITREP Section A Item 8 based on the WHtR framework.

### Five Directed Comments

The MARADMIN establishes five distinct directed comments depending on the MRO's body composition status.

1. Within WHtR standards.
2. Exceeded WHtR but within body fat standards (BIA or tape).
3. Exceeded WHtR and maximum body fat (adverse report).
4. Reevaluation waiver or exemption granted.
5. BCP assignment waiver granted.

### Section A Item 8 Field Entries

Per MARADMIN 066/26<Citation source="MARADMIN 066/26" /> paragraph 3.g.

- Item 8.d. (height) entered as "000."
- Item 8.e. (weight) entered as "000."
- Item 8.f. (body fat) left blank.

### Why This Matters for Every FITREP

Every FITREP submitted on or after 1 January 2026 must apply the new framework. Reporting Officials must select the correct directed comment based on the MRO's body composition status. Incorrect entries trigger return for correction.

The Body Composition Reporting on Performance Evaluations leaf covers MARADMIN 066/26<Citation source="MARADMIN 066/26" /> in detail.

## Common Questions Marines Ask

What is the difference between FITREP and JEPES?

FITREP per MCO 1610.7B<Citation source="MCO 1610.7B" /> applies to Sergeants and above plus all Officers. JEPES per MCO 1616.1<Citation source="MCO 1616.1" /> applies to E-1 through E-4. Both are performance evaluation systems but with different formats and stakeholders.

When did JEPES start?

MCO 1616.1<Citation source="MCO 1616.1" /> implemented JEPES for E-1 through E-4. The framework replaced the legacy proficiency and conduct (Pro/Con) marks system.

What is the Performance Evaluation Section (PES)?

The HQMC M&RA section that runs the performance evaluation program. Maintains MCO 1610.7B<Citation source="MCO 1610.7B" /> and MCO 1616.1<Citation source="MCO 1616.1" />. Operates the PERB. Email smb_hqmc_a-pes@usmc.mil.

How does MARADMIN 066/26<Citation source="MARADMIN 066/26" /> affect my FITREPs?

Per MARADMIN 066/26<Citation source="MARADMIN 066/26" /> effective 1 January 2026, body composition reporting on FITREPs uses the new WHtR framework. Section A Item 8 fields are entered as "000" or left blank. One of five directed comments is selected based on body composition status.

Who is my Reporting Senior?

The officer or SNCO who observes your performance during the reporting period. Typically your immediate commander or the next-level supervisor for SNCOs and Officers.

Who is my Reviewing Officer?

The senior officer above your RS. Reviews the RS's report and concurs or non-concurs.

What is the MBS?

The Master Brief Sheet. The aggregate display of all FITREPs in your OMPF. Selection boards, monitors, and reenlistment authorities review the MBS.

What is the PERB?

The Performance Evaluation Review Board at HQMC. Adjudicates substantive errors in FITREPs. Limited appeal grounds, time limits, specific petition format. The FITREP Appeals leaf covers the framework.

Can I appeal a JEPES report?

JEPES has its own redress process distinct from the PERB. Coordinate with the PES through your unit S-1 for current JEPES appeal procedures.

How do I see my own FITREPs?

Through the Marine Online (MOL) portal. The OMPF displays current and historical FITREPs. The MBS shows the aggregate profile.
