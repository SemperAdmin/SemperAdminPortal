## Role and responsibility

The lead command DCP runs the joint directive process per MCO 5215.1K<Citation source="MCO 5215.1K" /> Enclosure (1) Chapter 4 paragraph 1 (Introduction). The S-1 officer at the lead command signs by direction on the routing through partner commands. The S-1 chief tracks coordination through preliminary (Chapter 4 paragraph 7) and final (Chapter 4 paragraph 8) phases. The signature authorities at each partner command sign per Chapter 4 paragraph 9 and 10. Every partner command's DCP indexes the joint directive on issuance.

Source documents flow from the policy or procedural area requiring multi-principal action, the partner command identification, the preliminary coordination feedback, the final coordination signatures, the SSIC assignment per Chapter 4 paragraph 2, and the applicability scope per Chapter 4 paragraph 3. Joint directive outputs include the signed joint directive with the multi-principal signature block per Chapter 4 paragraph 10, the checklist of effective joint directives per Chapter 4 paragraph 6, the DCP index entries at every partner command, and the joint cancellation when applicable per Chapter 4 paragraph 4.

Per MCO 5215.1K Enclosure (1) Chapter 4 paragraph 1 (Introduction), a joint directive carries the signatures of two or more principals (Marine Corps and one or more partner services or agencies). Per paragraph 3 (Applicability), the joint directive applies to all signing principals' commands. Per paragraph 9 (Signature Authority), every principal signs per their command's signature authority rules.

## Trigger events

- Multi-command or multi-service policy or procedural area requires unified directive.
- Marine Corps and Navy area requires joint directive (e.g., naval-Marine joint policy).
- Marine Corps and another agency partnership requires formal directive.
- Existing joint directive expires or requires update.
- Cancellation of a joint directive routed for processing.
- Coordination breakdown identified during internal review.

## Processing workflow

1. Validate joint directive applicability. Per MCO 5215.1K<Citation source="MCO 5215.1K" /> Enclosure (1) Chapter 4 paragraph 3 (Applicability of Joint Directives), confirm the directive covers all signing principals.
2. Apply SSIC and signature requirements. Per Chapter 4 paragraph 2 (SSIC and Signature Requirements), assign the SSIC and the multi-principal signature requirement.
3. Build the joint directive content. Per Chapter 4 paragraph 5 (Information Required for Joint Directives), structure the content with the joint applicability paragraphs.
4. Maintain the checklist of effective joint directives. Per Chapter 4 paragraph 6 (Checklist of Effective Directives), the lead DCP maintains a joint-specific checklist.
5. Run preliminary coordination. Per Chapter 4 paragraph 7 (Preliminary Coordination), the lead DCP circulates the draft for partner command review.
6. Run final coordination. Per Chapter 4 paragraph 8 (Final Coordination), the lead DCP circulates the final draft for partner command signature.
7. Apply signature authority. Per Chapter 4 paragraph 9 (Signature Authority), every principal signs per their command's signature authority delegation.
8. Apply two-or-more-principal format. Per Chapter 4 paragraph 10 (Format of a Directive Signed by Two or More Principals), the signature block accommodates every signing principal.
9. Process cancellation. Per Chapter 4 paragraph 4 (Canceling Joint Directives), the cancellation routes through every signing principal command.
10. Distribute the joint directive. Route through the lead DCP and each partner command's DCP per the distribution list.

- DON Directives Issuance System. Surfaces Navy and DON-level joint directives.
- Checklist of Effective Joint Directives. Per Chapter 4 paragraph 6.

## Common pitfalls

- Preliminary coordination skipped or rushed. Per MCO 5215.1K<Citation source="MCO 5215.1K" /> Enclosure (1) Chapter 4 paragraph 7, the preliminary coordination identifies partner command objections early. Skipping the phase risks a stalled final coordination.
- Final coordination signature missing one principal. Per Chapter 4 paragraph 8, every principal signs. A missing signature voids the joint directive.
- Cancellation processed without partner concurrence. Per Chapter 4 paragraph 4, cancellation routes through every signing principal. Unilateral cancellation breaks the joint authority.
- SSIC assignment conflicts across partner commands. Per Chapter 4 paragraph 2, the SSIC is consistent across the signing partners. Reconcile at draft.
- Applicability paragraph excludes a signing principal command. Per Chapter 4 paragraph 3, the applicability covers every signing principal. Validate at draft.

## Decision points

- Joint directive versus separate single-principal directives. Per MCO 5215.1K<Citation source="MCO 5215.1K" /> Enclosure (1) Chapter 4 paragraphs 1 and 3, when the same policy applies to multiple principals with coordinated implementation, the joint directive is the right path. Separate directives suit independent implementation.
- Lead command selection. The lead command runs the DCP process. The partner commands signal preliminary and final coordination.
- Cancellation routing path. Per Chapter 4 paragraph 4, cancellation requires joint action. The lead command drafts the cancellation memorandum and circulates for partner signatures.
